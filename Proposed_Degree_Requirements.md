# Computer Science (BS) — Degree Structure Draft

**Total Hours:** 74

This document presents a structured Computer Science BS degree organized around enduring abstractions, coherent progression, and applied professional preparation for students pursuing a **terminal bachelor's degree**.

---

## One-Page Rationale

### Computer Science as the Science of Abstraction

Computer Science is fundamentally the science of **abstraction**: identifying essential structure, ignoring irrelevant detail, and building systems that scale in complexity, performance, and reliability. Programming languages, algorithms, operating systems, databases, networks, and AI systems are all expressions of layered abstractions.

Because the technologies used in computing change rapidly, this degree is intentionally designed to emphasize **durable conceptual abstractions** rather than short-lived tools or platforms. Students are taught models, paradigms, and design principles that will remain relevant over the next 10–15 years, even as specific frameworks, libraries, and languages evolve.

The curriculum prioritizes:
- Algorithmic and computational abstractions
- Systems and resource abstractions
- Data and information abstractions
- Programming language and software architecture abstractions
- AI and decision-making abstractions
- Ethical and societal abstractions surrounding computing systems

By grounding students in these abstractions, the program equips graduates to learn new technologies quickly, evaluate tradeoffs, and adapt to unfamiliar problem domains.

---

### A Terminal BS with Professional Intent

This degree is explicitly designed for students pursuing a **terminal Bachelor of Science in Computer Science**, not as a pre-professional pipeline to graduate research. The program emphasizes:

- Breadth across major areas of computing
- Integration of theory, systems, and applications
- Ethical reasoning and communication
- Real-world experience through a required capstone or experiential component

Graduates are prepared to enter industry, startups, public service, or mission-driven organizations as capable, adaptable computing professionals who can grow with their field.

Depth is encouraged, but specialization is intentionally limited to ensure graduates remain flexible and broadly capable in a rapidly changing profession.
