# Capstone Class — Draft Justification

**Status note:** Capstone experiences currently exist within the Software Engineering and Machine Learning emphases, incorporating both company partnerships and student-designed projects. This document outlines a scalable capstone class model that can serve as a required experiential learning option within the proposed degree structure.

## Purpose in the Proposed Degree Structure

The capstone class serves as one pathway to fulfill the **required experiential learning component** that bridges the junior core and advanced electives. It provides students with an integrative project experience that synthesizes knowledge across multiple areas of the CS curriculum, preparing them for complex, team-based professional work.

The capstone class is designed to be the **primary "catch-all" option** for students who do not obtain internships or find suitable undergraduate research experiences, ensuring that all students can complete the experiential learning requirement.

## Proposed Capstone Model for Scalability

To accommodate the experiential learning requirement at scale, the capstone class should follow a structured, faculty-guided model:

### Project Structure

- **Faculty-designed "canned" projects**: Full-time faculty members design **two to three comprehensive projects** that intentionally incorporate multiple elements of the CS curriculum (algorithms, systems, databases, AI, software engineering, ethics).
- **Small team collaboration**: Students select one of the available projects and work in small teams (typically 3-5 students) to complete it over the semester.
- **Cross-curricular integration**: Projects are explicitly designed to require students to apply knowledge from the junior core and synthesize concepts across different computing domains.

### Staffing and Management

- **Professional adjunct management**: One or more professional adjuncts with industry experience manage the course, overseeing team dynamics, providing technical guidance, and evaluating student work.
- **Faculty project ownership**: Full-time faculty who design the projects provide initial framing, learning objectives, and technical specifications, but the day-to-day management is delegated to adjuncts.

### Rationale for This Model

This approach provides several advantages:

1. **Scalability**: Pre-designed projects with adjunct management allow the capstone to serve large cohorts of students without overwhelming faculty resources.
2. **Consistency**: Faculty-designed projects ensure all students engage with integrative, curriculum-aligned challenges regardless of team or section.
3. **Quality control**: Faculty oversight of project design maintains academic rigor while adjunct management provides practical, professional-oriented mentorship.
4. **Accessibility**: As a "catch-all" option, the capstone ensures no student is excluded from experiential learning due to lack of internship or research opportunities.

## Alignment with BYU Aims

The capstone class advances BYU Aims through integrative, team-based project work:

### Intellectually Enlarging

- **Cross-curricular synthesis**: Students integrate algorithms, systems, databases, AI, and software engineering principles to solve complex, realistic problems.
- **Problem decomposition and design**: Students apply abstractions learned in the junior core to design, implement, and evaluate multi-component systems.
- **Critical evaluation**: Teams assess tradeoffs, justify design decisions, and reflect on the effectiveness of their solutions.

### Character Building

- **Teamwork and collaboration**: Students navigate group dynamics, distribute work fairly, and hold each other accountable—skills essential to professional practice.
- **Responsibility and ownership**: Each team member must contribute meaningfully to the project's success, developing habits of reliability and initiative.
- **Ethical reasoning**: Faculty-designed projects incorporate ethical dimensions (privacy, accessibility, bias, impact), requiring students to reason about responsible technology design.

### Lifelong Learning and Service

- **Adaptive problem-solving**: Students encounter unfamiliar technical challenges, requiring them to research solutions, learn new tools, and iterate on designs—skills central to professional growth.
- **Reflection on learning**: Students identify strengths, gaps, and areas for future development, connecting the capstone experience to ongoing professional preparation.
- **Service-oriented projects**: Projects can be framed around meaningful applications that address real needs, helping students see how their skills can contribute to the broader community.

## Learning Outcomes

The capstone class shares the core experiential learning outcomes while emphasizing integrative, team-based work. At the end of the course, students will be able to:

- Apply academic knowledge from multiple areas of the CS curriculum to solve complex, real-world problems.
- Articulate how courses in the junior core prepared them for integrative project work.
- Collaborate effectively in small teams to design, implement, and deliver a substantial computing system.
- Identify areas where they need further development in their academic training and frame future coursework in the context of professional preparation.
- Demonstrate the ability to learn dynamically and adapt to solve evolving problems in a deadline-driven environment.
- Share real experiences and self-introspection in facing and overcoming difficult, hard-to-solve problems.
- Understand which aspects of their field of discipline they enjoy and want to pursue long-term, and in what capacity.

## Why Capstone Class Belongs in the Experiential Learning Requirement

Including a capstone class as a required option ensures:

1. **Universal access**: All students can complete the experiential learning requirement, regardless of internship availability or research opportunities.
2. **Curricular integration**: Faculty-designed projects ensure students synthesize knowledge across the junior core and apply abstractions in realistic contexts.
3. **Team-based professional preparation**: Students develop collaboration, communication, and project management skills essential for industry and mission-driven work.
4. **Scalability**: The structured model allows the program to serve large cohorts without disproportionate faculty workload.

The capstone class pathway complements internship and research options by providing a controlled, curriculum-aligned integrative experience that prepares students for the complexity and teamwork demands of professional computing practice.

## Building on Existing Capstone Experiences

The proposed capstone class builds on the department's existing expertise:

- **Software Engineering emphasis**: Demonstrated success with company partnerships and structured project experiences.
- **Machine Learning emphasis**: Experience with student-designed and faculty-guided projects that integrate AI and data systems.

The new capstone model adapts these proven approaches into a scalable, core-curriculum-aligned offering that can serve as the primary experiential learning pathway for the majority of students.
