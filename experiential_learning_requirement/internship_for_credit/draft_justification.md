# CS Internship for Credit — Draft Justification

**Status note:** The CS Internship for Credit program already exists as CS 199R and has demonstrated significant value in student preparation and professional development. This document frames its inclusion as a required experiential learning option within the proposed degree structure, emphasizing alignment with BYU Aims.

## Purpose in the Proposed Degree Structure

The internship for credit serves as one pathway to fulfill the **required experiential learning component** that bridges the junior core and advanced electives. It provides students with supervised professional experience that integrates academic knowledge with real-world computing practice, preparing them for adaptive, ethical, and lifelong professional growth.

## Alignment with BYU Aims

The internship for credit program advances all dimensions of the BYU Aims, with particular emphasis on **character building** and **lifelong learning and service**.

### Character Building

Professional internships cultivate the habits, virtues, and accountability essential to ethical computing practice:

- **Integrity and professionalism**: Students work under real constraints, deadlines, and expectations, learning to deliver reliable work and take ownership of their contributions.
- **Collaboration and humility**: Working with experienced professionals and peers requires listening, adapting, and valuing others' expertise.
- **Resilience and problem-solving**: Facing unfamiliar, complex problems in dynamic environments builds perseverance, resourcefulness, and the confidence to tackle hard challenges.
- **Ethical reasoning in context**: Students encounter real tradeoffs involving privacy, security, accessibility, and user impact, developing the judgment to recognize and navigate ethical questions in professional settings.

These experiences shape students' professional character in ways that classroom instruction alone cannot replicate.

### Lifelong Learning and Service

Internships prepare students to grow continuously and contribute meaningfully throughout their careers:

- **Adaptive learning**: Students encounter technologies, tools, and problem domains not covered in coursework, requiring them to learn independently and dynamically—skills central to lifelong professional growth.
- **Self-awareness and direction**: By reflecting on what aspects of computing they enjoy and excel at, students gain clarity about how they can best serve others through their work.
- **Professional networks and mentorship**: Building relationships with mentors and colleagues creates pathways for continued learning, collaboration, and service opportunities beyond graduation.
- **Real-world impact**: Students see how their technical skills can address genuine needs, solve meaningful problems, and contribute to organizations, communities, and individuals—connecting their work to a broader sense of purpose and service.

### Intellectually Enlarging

Internships expand students' intellectual capacity by:

- **Applying abstractions to novel contexts**: Students use algorithms, systems thinking, data modeling, and software design principles learned in the junior core to solve unfamiliar, open-ended problems.
- **Recognizing knowledge gaps**: Real-world experience reveals areas where students need deeper understanding, motivating intentional learning in subsequent coursework and self-directed study.
- **Bridging theory and practice**: Students gain appreciation for how foundational concepts inform practical decisions, deepening their conceptual understanding and professional judgment.

### Spiritually Strengthening

The internship experience can strengthen students spiritually by:

- **Connecting work to purpose**: Students reflect on how their talents and training can be used to bless others, aligning their professional identity with values of service and stewardship.
- **Navigating ethical complexity with integrity**: Facing real ethical dilemmas invites students to consider their principles and the moral dimensions of technology, fostering moral growth and discernment.

## Learning Outcomes

The experiential learning outcomes already articulated for the CS internship program align directly with both the proposed degree structure and BYU Aims. At the end of the course, students will be able to:

- Apply academic knowledge from the classroom to real-world problems.
- Articulate the value of courses in the program in preparing them for professional experience.
- Differentiate themselves from other students with employers through professional experience in their field of discipline.
- Identify areas where they need further development in their academic training and frame future coursework in the context of professional preparation.
- Demonstrate the ability to learn dynamically and adapt to solve evolving problems in a deadline-driven environment.
- Ask faculty and mentors for strong references for future employment.
- Share real experiences and self-introspection in facing and overcoming difficult, hard-to-solve problems.
- Understand which aspects of their field of discipline they enjoy and want to pursue long-term, and in what capacity.

## Why Internship for Credit Belongs in the Experiential Learning Requirement

Including internship for credit as a required option ensures:

1. **Professional readiness**: Graduates enter the workforce with demonstrated ability to apply computing knowledge in professional contexts.
2. **Character formation**: Students develop the habits, judgment, and resilience needed for ethical, effective professional practice.
3. **Informed specialization**: Real-world experience helps students make better choices about breadth and depth electives, capstone projects, and career paths.
4. **Lifelong learning foundation**: Students learn how to learn independently, adapt to new technologies, and seek mentorship—skills that sustain professional growth for decades.

The internship for credit pathway embodies the proposed degree's emphasis on **terminal BS professional preparation** by ensuring students graduate not only with conceptual foundations but also with proven capacity to grow, adapt, and serve through computing.

## Scalability and Staffing

A key practical consideration for making internship for credit a required experiential learning option is program scalability. Working with Bradley Beal, we estimate that **one adjunct instructor can effectively manage approximately 100 students** participating in the internship for credit program.

This management model requires distributed effort across multiple terms:

- **Winter semester**: Helping students identify, apply for, and secure internship opportunities
- **Spring/Summer terms**: Managing reflective learning exercises, reviewing student progress reports, and coordinating employer feedback reports

This staffing model demonstrates that the internship for credit pathway can scale to accommodate a required experiential learning component without disproportionate resource demands, making it a sustainable option for the revised degree structure.

## Research Evidence

Research on CS/engineering student internships supports the distinctive value of this experiential pathway. Minnes, Ghanbari Serslev, and Edwards (2020) found that while both internships and capstone classes produce similar learning outcomes, **internships uniquely develop students' understanding of the importance of professional networking**—a critical dimension of lifelong learning and career development.

This finding reinforces the value of offering internship for credit as one pathway within the required experiential learning component, complementing capstone and research options with its unique contribution to professional development and career preparation.

**Reference:**  
Minnes, Mia, Sheena Ghanbari Serslev, and Madison Edwards. "Attitude shifts and transformation during Computer Science and Engineering Student Internships." *2020 ASEE Virtual Annual Conference Content* (2020).
