# Experiential Learning Requirement

The experiential learning requirement ensures every student applies core CS knowledge in a supervised, real-world setting before graduation.

## Addressing Critical Gaps and Building Character

This requirement addresses significant gaps revealed in the 2025 Unit Review:
- **Only 15% of students currently engage in RA opportunities** — our requirement ensures 100% of students participate in high-impact experiential learning
- **77% of undergraduates receive mentoring** (vs. 97% of graduates) — this requirement guarantees all students receive structured mentorship
- **40% of graduates leave without full-time job offers** — hands-on experience builds recruitability and portfolio items that directly address student concerns about converting coursework to résumé qualifications

### Character Building Through Professional Experience
More profoundly, this requirement supports **character building** by placing students in situations where:
- Professional responsibility matters: Their work is reviewed by employers, mentors, or faculty in genuine contexts, not sandbox environments
- Teamwork and communication are essential: Capstone projects require coordination; internships demand professional communication; research requires collaboration
- Ethical decision-making is necessary: Real-world projects involve tradeoffs between complexity, cost, security, and user impact—decisions that shape character
- Reflection and growth occur: Mentored experiences include explicit reflection on learning, mistakes, and professional development

### Lifelong Learning Habits
The requirement also instills habits of **lifelong learning and service** by:
- Creating relationships with mentors (faculty, supervisors, colleagues) who model continuous learning
- Requiring structured reflection on what was learned and how to continue learning independently
- Placing students in professional contexts where they see scientists and engineers solving novel problems, not just practitioners executing known solutions
- Building confidence in tackling unfamiliar technical challenges, essential for careers spanning decades of technological change

## Experiential Learning Outcomes

At the end of the experience, an engaged and motivated student will be able to:

- Apply academic knowledge from the classroom to real-world problems.
- Articulate the value of courses in the program in preparing them for professional experience.
- Differentiate themselves from other students with employers through professional experience in their field of discipline.
- Identify areas where they need further development in their academic training and frame future coursework in the context of professional preparation.
- Demonstrate the ability to learn dynamically and adapt to solve evolving problems in a deadline-driven environment.
- Ask faculty and mentors for strong references for future employment.
- Share real experiences and self-introspection in facing and overcoming difficult, hard-to-solve problems.
- Understand which aspects of their field of discipline they enjoy and want to pursue long-term, and in what capacity.

These learning outcomes are compatible with BYU's guidelines on [experiential learning](https://experience.byu.edu/). BYU's experiential learning [framework](BYU-experiential-learning-framework.pdf) and [faculty guidelines](BYU-experiential-learning-faculty-guide.pdf) are included in the supporting material in this repo.

## Implementation Capacity and Feasibility

**The Current Gap:**
The 2025 Unit Review revealed that:
- **85% of students currently do NOT engage in research or RA opportunities** (only 15% do) — the proposed requirement brings this to 100%
- **23% of undergraduates do NOT receive faculty mentoring** (only 77% do) — the proposed requirement ensures all students have structured mentorship
- **40% of graduates leave without full-time job offers** — experiential learning directly addresses this through portfolio-building and professional development

**Scalability Through Capstone and Internship Models:**
The proposal scales this requirement through two primary pathways designed for systemic sustainability:

**Capstone courses** are scalable because:
- Adjunc faculty teach pre-designed integrative courses to cohorts of students (typically 30–50 per course)
- Multiple adjunct faculty can teach capstone simultaneously with minimal coordination overhead
- Capstone doesn't require external partnerships; it uses internal department expertise

**Internship for credit (CS 199R)** is scalable given:
- BYU CS has a strong pool of capable **adjunct faculty and industry professionals** who can supervise and assess internship experiences
- The previously-conducted two-year internship experiment (documented in [internship_for_credit/](internship_for_credit/)) demonstrates both feasibility and positive student outcomes
- Industry partnerships for internship placements already exist and can expand incrementally

**Research pathway expansion** addresses the remaining capacity:
- Current RA positions (15% engagement) can expand modestly if faculty are willing to create structured research experiences that can be given to multiple undergraduate students and are not on critical path for current papers
- Faculty mentoring infrastructure is already strong, as noted in the unit review: "Students with RA positions spoke highly of professors' willingness to support experiential learning"

The three pathways provide flexibility: students can choose capstone (faculty-mentored), internship (industry-supervised), or research (faculty-mentored) based on their goals and circumstances, distributing load across multiple capacity sources rather than relying on any single bottleneck.

## Student Support During Scale-Up

To achieve 100% participation while maintaining quality:

- **Career development workshops** integrated into junior core courses to help students identify appropriate pathways (capstone, internship or research) based on their interests, constraints, and career goals
- **Faculty mentor training programs** to improve research mentoring consistency and prepare faculty to supervise undergraduate researchers effectively
- **Industry recruitment support** from external relations and career services to expand internship placement opportunities
- **Clear messaging** to students that they have genuine agency in *which* pathway (capstone/internship/research) best fits their circumstances and preferences—all three pathways are equally valid and valued

## Note on Internship for Credit Models

There are two models for internship for credit: the model described in [experiential_learning_requirement/internship_for_credit/](experiential_learning_requirement/internship_for_credit/), and a cooperative model based on long-standing partnerships with companies who commit to providing a set number of internships for our students each year. Ryan Farrell has gathered information on the coop-based model. Further discussions are needed to determine how to best reconcile the two models. One option is to begin with the model described in [experiential_learning_requirement/internship_for_credit/](experiential_learning_requirement/internship_for_credit/) while working toward the coop model, since the coop model will require more effort before it could be deployed.

The materials in [experiential_learning_requirement/internship_for_credit/](experiential_learning_requirement/internship_for_credit/) document the justification and results of a two-year experiment run by the External Relations Committee that never received approval for a faculty-wide discussion.
