# More Granular Summary of Survey Concerns

## Overview
This document summarizes common concerns raised in survey responses and follow-up feedback regarding the proposed CS degree changes. It incorporates both quantitative trends and qualitative feedback, including detailed faculty input.

---

## 1. Junior Core Structure and Content

### A. Course Selection
- Concern that databases and programming languages may be overrepresented
- Perception of a systems-heavy core
- Possible underemphasis on algorithms and computational problem solving

### B. Cohort Model
- Suggestion to implement a cohort-based junior core
- Benefits:
  - Stronger student connection
  - Improved mentoring and retention
- Tradeoff:
  - Reduced exposure to diverse peers

### C. Course Granularity
- Critique of 3-credit course rigidity
- Suggested alternatives:
  - Modular (1–2 credit) units
  - Topic-based composition instead of fixed courses

### D. Design Transparency
- Need for clearer explanation of:
  - Why specific courses were selected
  - How they align with learning objectives

---

## 2. AI Integration

### A. Identified Gap
- Lack of required coursework focused on practical AI usage

### B. Desired Outcomes
- Students should learn:
  - Prompting and tool use
  - Human-AI collaboration
  - Limitations and failure modes

### C. Proposal
- Add 1–3 required AI-focused or AI-integrated courses

---

## 3. Breadth/Depth Structure

### A. Enrollment Concerns
- Too many 400-level courses
- Potential imbalance:
  - Over-enrollment in breadth
  - Under-enrollment in depth

### B. Category Design
- Questions about:
  - Number and definition of categories
  - Grouping of unrelated areas

### C. Demand Mismatch
- Some courses historically low demand
- Concern about forced redistribution of students

### D. Alternatives
- Merge all electives into breadth groups
- Require distribution across groups rather than separate depth

### E. Missing Courses
- Example: CS 393 not clearly included
- General lack of clarity on inclusion/exclusion decisions

---

## 4. Balance of Topics

### A. Systems vs Theory
- Approximately 19 credits in systems/software in the core
- Concern about insufficient focus on:
  - Algorithms
  - Computational thinking

### B. Math Foundations
- Agreement on importance of math
- Disagreement on:
  - Which courses
  - Flexibility and alternatives
  - Relevance to CS

---

## 5. Experiential Learning

### A. Strengths
- Widely supported
- Seen as critical for:
  - Real-world preparation
  - Career readiness

### B. Concerns
- 3 credits is insufficient
- Suggestion:
  - Multi-semester co-op model (~8 months)

### C. Employment Impact
- High percentage of students without jobs at graduation
- Experiential learning seen as key improvement lever

### D. Mission-Oriented Work
- Desire for projects that:
  - Serve underserved populations
  - Align with broader institutional mission

---

## 6. Course Selection Transparency

### Recurring Concern
- Lack of explanation for:
  - Core course choices
  - Elective group composition

### Implication
- Need to:
  - Document decision criteria
  - Acknowledge tradeoffs

---

## 7. Structural Flexibility

### Concerns
- Rigid course structures
- Fixed categories

### Suggested Direction
- Modular curriculum design
- Flexible pathways
- Adaptation to student needs and industry trends

---

## Meta-Themes

### 1. Vision vs Execution
- Strong agreement with overall vision
- Concerns focus on implementation

### 2. Modernization
- Need for:
  - AI integration
  - Industry alignment
  - Experiential learning

### 3. Practical Constraints
- Enrollment balancing
- Faculty capacity
- Course availability

### 4. Communication Gaps
- Need for clearer articulation of:
  - Character-building outcomes
  - Design decisions

---

## Bottom Line
Feedback is constructive and focused on refinement. Key areas for improvement include:
1. Junior core structure and balance
2. AI integration
3. Breadth/depth redesign
4. Experiential learning expansion
5. Transparency in decision-making
