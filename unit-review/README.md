# Unit Review

The following excerpts highlight pivotal findings from the 2025 Computer Science Unit Review and show direct alignment with the proposed curriculum changes.

These key findings highlight critical gaps in our current degree structure and provide strong impetus for comprehensive curricular reform, specifically the need for clearer core competencies, more intentional integration of theoretical and practical skills, and meaningful experiential learning across the program.

### Recommendation: _Streamline and clarify core requirements for the general computer science degree._ (pp.5,6)

> '[T]he general computer science degree is still the most populated degree by undergraduate students ... [but] the 'core CS degree is not receiving adequate attention' and is lacking 'a clearly defined set of core competencies'' (p.6).

**Connection to proposal:** This directly motivates our restructuring to explicitly define core competencies through CS Foundations, a structured Junior Core, and required breadth/depth elective areas.

> 'We would recommend a thorough review of the general computer science degree with the intent of **strengthening the program** and **trimming the number of elective courses offered**' (p.6).

**Connection to proposal:** Our proposal addresses this by consolidating scattered electives into four focused breadth groups while maintaining sufficient choice for student interests.

> 'Emphasis should be given to provide both **theoretical scope** ... and **practical experience** necessary for entrance into the industry' (p.6).

**Connection to proposal:** Our curriculum balances theory (algorithms, systems, languages, databases) with required experiential learning (capstone, internship, or research) to emphasize industry readiness for students seeking a terminal B.S. degree.

### Recommendation: _Strengthen experiential learning and professional development_ (p. 10)

> 'The department might consider increasing the percentage of RA opportunities and/or ensuring that more inspiring learning opportunities encourage reflection and professionalization under the direction of a faculty member' (p. 10).

**Connection to proposal:** Our experiential learning requirement mandates that every student engage in capstone, internship, or undergraduate research with faculty mentorship, promoting structured reflection, skill building, and professional development.

**Supporting evidence from review:** Critical gaps currently exist in student engagement:
- Only **15% of students currently engage in RA opportunities** (Self-Study, p. 19)
- Only **77% of undergraduates receive mentoring that encourages learning**, compared to **97% of graduates** (Student Survey, p. 3)

Our proposal directly addresses these gaps by making experiential learning **required for all students**, not optional, ensuring universal access to mentored professional development.

### Additional Finding: _Job Placement and Resume-Building Skills_

> 'Computer science has historically enjoyed high job placement; however, with recent changes in industry and government, jobs have become less plentiful. Currently, only about **40% of CS majors leave with a full-time job**.' (p. 9)

> 'Some students connected this low job placement to their difficulty in converting assignments in class to **résumé line items**.' (Student Interviews; Student Surveys, pp. 16, 19, 22, 24, 25, 27, 29, 32)

**Connection to proposal:** This reinforces the critical need for curriculum to balance theoretical depth with **practical, portfolio-building experience**. Our junior core courses are designed so that assignments directly translate to professional portfolios. Our required experiential learning ensures students build concrete, resume-ready artifacts through capstone projects, structured internships, or research contributions.

### Additional Finding: _Elective Course Proliferation_

> 'Every research faculty hire is expected to design two new courses, creating a proliferation of elective courses that often have low enrollment and may overlap with one another and thus may not be the most efficient use of department resources.' (External Report, pp. 3-4)

> 'We would recommend a thorough review of the general computer science degree...**trimming the number of elective courses offered**.' (p. 6)

**Connection to proposal:** Our breadth-depth model consolidates scattered electives into **four focused breadth areas** (AI/ML, HCI/Graphics, Systems/Software Engineering, Theory/Algorithms), eliminating redundancy while maintaining meaningful student choice and clarifying the conceptual structure of upper-level coursework.
