# Concerns and Proposed Responses

This document merges the prioritized concerns with the corresponding proposed responses into a single reference.

## In No Particular Order

## 1. AI Integration in the Curriculum

### Concern
AI integration is viewed as essential for long-term relevance, but the current required structure is seen as insufficient.

- Faculty identified a gap in required AI-integrated coursework.
- There is demand for practical AI skills, including prompting and human-AI collaboration.
- Some feedback emphasized that students need stronger preparation for AI-shaped career paths.
- Respondents expressed concern that AI literacy should be distributed more broadly in the curriculum.

### Response
The proposed response is to prioritize AI integration across courses while avoiding a full immediate redesign.

- The committee agrees AI is critically important.
- A complete curriculum redesign around AI is considered outside the current charge.
- The AI-across-curriculum direction is intended to distribute AI competencies beyond a single course.
- The proposed AI course is positioned as foundational (abstractions, agents, hybrid systems), not the sole AI literacy mechanism.

## 2. Breadth/Depth Structure Design

### Concern
The breadth/depth model is generally supported in concept, but its current category design is seen as too rigid and somewhat arbitrary.

- Faculty raised concerns about artificial grouping boundaries.
- Some noted misalignment between category choices and student demand.
- The number and placement of 400-level courses were questioned.
- Respondents asked for a more flexible or adaptable elective structure.

### Response
The proposed response is to treat current categories as a provisional baseline that will be refined over time.

- The committee describes the current structure as intentional but not permanent.
- Categories are expected to evolve with faculty input and field changes.
- Future updates should account for student needs and enrollment realities.
- The present structure is framed as a practical starting point rather than a final taxonomy.

## 3. Implementation Details, Resource Planning, and Enrollment Viability

### Concern
The overall direction is seen as promising, but implementation and enrollment planning are currently under-specified.

- Faculty want clearer course-level scope and sequencing.
- Questions remain around who will own and staff major components.
- Respondents highlighted uncertainty about scheduling, demand balancing, and sustainable rollout.
- There is concern that uneven enrollments could strain resources and reduce viability in some offerings.

### Response
The proposed response is to pair phased implementation planning with active enrollment monitoring and coordinated adjustments.

- The committee agrees that implementation specifics should be made more explicit.
- Documentation should include sequencing and staffing plans.
- A milestone-based rollout over the next three years is recommended.
- Offerings should be rebalanced as needed to maintain viability and align with realistic demand.

## 4. Mentoring Capacity and Quality Control in Experiential Learning

### Concern
Experiential learning is strongly supported, but faculty are concerned about mentoring scale and consistency.

- Respondents noted that mentorship quality may vary across pathways.
- Capacity constraints could limit scalability without additional support.
- Faculty called for clearer expectations and supervision standards.
- There is concern that uneven mentoring could produce uneven student outcomes.

### Response
The proposed response is to scale experiential pathways gradually with shared standards and quality controls.

- The committee acknowledges mentoring capacity as a core operational issue.
- Common expectations for mentoring should be established across pathways.
- Minimum supervision and feedback standards should be defined.
- Quality assurance checkpoints should be used before scaling further.

## 5. Course Selection Transparency (Core, Including Math)

### Concern
Faculty want clearer justification for why specific core and math courses were selected.

- Respondents requested explicit rationale and tradeoff documentation.
- Some feedback indicated uncertainty about selection criteria.
- Questions remain about how decisions map to long-term program goals.
- Lack of transparency can reduce buy-in even when outcomes are reasonable.

### Response
The proposed response is to preserve current selections for now while strengthening documentation of decision rationale.

- The committee states current decisions were based on faculty judgment and observed trends.
- Existing choices are also informed by published computing-education perspectives.
- Additional systematic documentation is needed to clarify reasoning.
- Better visibility into tradeoffs is expected to improve trust and alignment.

## 6. Experiential Learning Credit Limit (3-Credit Cap)

### Concern
The 3-credit cap is viewed by some as too limited to deliver the full intended impact of experiential learning.

- Respondents argued that meaningful experiential work may require more scope than one 3-credit unit.
- Some suggested expanded credits or multi-term participation models.
- Faculty concern focused on whether current scope matches desired outcomes.
- The issue is framed as a practical constraint rather than opposition to experiential learning itself.

### Response
The proposed response is to start with a feasible 3-credit model and revisit scope as capacity and evidence develop.

- The committee acknowledges the concern and sees value in potentially broader scope.
- The current design is intentionally evolutionary and constrained by near-term feasibility.
- Future iterations can increase credit or duration if quality and capacity support expansion.
- The present model is treated as a starting point rather than a fixed endpoint.
