# Faculty Survey Results

There are 22 total respondents.

## Executive Summary for each category

### Vision Statement

- **General**: 20 generally support, 2 do not support
- **Strengths**: The feedback strongly supports a focus on abstraction, durable concepts, and balancing theory with practice as central to computer science education. Respondents also value the emphasis on long-term understanding, practical skills, and encouraging lifelong learning through practices like actively seeking feedback.
- **Weaknesses**: The feedback raises concerns about whether the proposed direction adequately prepares students for industry, graduate school, and emerging fields like AI, with some questioning an overemphasis on abstraction and theory. While the overall direction is seen as promising, respondents highlight the need for clearer definitions, balance with practical problem-solving, and inclusion of broader educational goals like character development and lifelong learning.

### Junior Core

 - **General**: 19 generally support, 3 do not support
 - **Strengths**: The junior core is viewed as a strong and practical addition, building on existing structures while remaining easy to implement and aligned with a data-driven world. It is seen as providing a robust, traditional foundation in computer science, with the cohort model offering meaningful potential to enhance student learning and engagement.
 - **Weaknesses**: The feedback raises concerns about specific course choices in the junior core, particularly the role and relevance of CS 370 (AI/ML), CS 330, and CS 352, with some questioning whether alternatives like CS 270 or newer topics like agentic computing would be more appropriate. Respondents also note gaps such as the lack of security coverage, unclear course content and structure (especially for 352), and insufficient justification for why these particular courses form the core.

### Math Foundation

- **General**: 21 generally support, 1 does not support
- **Strengths**: The proposed math requirements are seen as providing a strong foundation in mathematical reasoning, with courses like Stat 240 (or possibly Stat 220) adding valuable statistical perspective alongside existing offerings. Respondents also appreciate maintaining familiar core classes while removing physics, and view the overall set as a broad, well-motivated base for modern, data-driven computer science.
- **Weaknesses**: While the math requirements are generally seen as strong, some respondents suggest refinements such as adding Math 290, reconsidering the requirement of Stat 240, or allowing flexible alternatives like Stat 220, 121, or 201. 

### Electives
- **General**: 17 generally support, 5 do not support
- **Strengths**: The elective structure is viewed positively, with respondents supporting the overall concept for general CS students and expressing clear approval of the idea. They also appreciate the breadth and depth approach and the strong coverage of topics it provides.
- **Weaknesses**: While the elective structure is generally well-received, there is a consistent concern about the lack of a clear, coherent rationale for how categories and courses are organized, including why specific areas and courses (e.g., CS 356 but not CS 256) were chosen. Respondents also note issues such as hidden prerequisites, uneven topic representation (e.g., limited security and HCI, narrow theory options), and a need for broader and more AI/LLM-aligned coverage.

### Experiential Learning
- **General**: 21 generally support, 1 does not support
- **Strengths**: Experiential learning is strongly supported as a much-needed addition, seen as an effective way to enhance learning—especially in the context of AI-assisted tools. It is valued for providing mentorship, real-world experience, and interpersonal development through capstones, internships, and research.
- **Weaknesses**: While experiential learning is strongly valued, a key concern is whether it can scale, given limits in faculty mentorship, staffing, and access to quality internships or capstones. Respondents also note challenges with implementation, including approval workload, consistency of outcomes, and credit structure.

## General Observations from Weaknesses Comments

### Lack of clear rationale and coherence in design
The curriculum lacks a clearly articulated rationale for why specific courses and structures were chosen—for example, why CS 330, CS 352, and CS 370 are in the junior core, or why electives are divided into four categories—making it difficult to see how the design aligns with the stated vision.

### Scalability, resource constraints, and consistency of student experience
The proposed structure may not scale given current faculty capacity, particularly for mentorship-heavy components like research, internships, and capstones, making it difficult to provide all students with high-quality opportunities. As a result, experiences may vary widely in rigor and value, leading to inconsistent student outcomes.

### Misalignment with industry trends and modern CS (especially AI)
The curriculum underemphasizes modern areas such as AI/ML, agent-based systems, and security—for example, requiring CS 370 (focused on traditional AI topics like search and planning) instead of or over alternatives like CS 270 (intro to ML), and lacking coverage of agent engineering or LLM-based systems.

### Course selection, structure, and level appropriateness
Some required courses do not clearly align with goals or level expectations—for example, CS 352 (formerly 452) may be too advanced for a 300-level course, and there are questions about the importance of CS 330, CS 370, and requiring Stat 240 versus alternatives like Stat 220.

### Flexibility in curriculum structure
The structure is overly rigid, requiring fixed elective categories and specific courses rather than allowing alternatives (e.g., Stat 220 vs. 240, CS 270 vs. 370), and enforcing predefined tracks that may not reflect the diversity of areas like HCI, graphics, and systems.

### Imbalance between theory, practice, breadth, and depth
The curriculum leans too heavily toward abstraction and theory at the expense of practical problem-solving, while also raising questions about whether requirements like additional math (e.g., Stat 240) and broad electives strike the right balance between depth and breadth.
