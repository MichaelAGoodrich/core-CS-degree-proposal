# Prioritized Concerns and Proposed Responses

## 1. AI Integration in the Curriculum

**Response Strategy: Important, but beyond scope of current charge**

We agree that AI integration is critically important. However, the committee felt that a full redesign of the curriculum around AI is beyond the scope of the charge we were given.

AI should be integrated across the curriculum, not isolated in a single course. A proposed framework for doing this is outlined in the AI-across-curriculum folder.

The proposed AI course is not intended to broadly address AI literacy. Instead, it provides:
- Abstractions for reasoning about AI systems
- Foundations for understanding agents and hybrid systems
- Tools for formal reasoning about multi-component AI systems

An agentics-focused course could:
- Be added to the systems breadth pool, or
- Be integrated across multiple courses

---

## 2. Breadth/Depth Structure Design

**Response Strategy: Intentional but provisional structure**

The current categories were based on historical enrollment trends and existing structure. These choices are somewhat arbitrary and are not intended to be permanent.

These categories will need to evolve over time based on:
- Enrollment patterns
- Faculty input
- Changes in the field

---

## 3. Junior Core Balance (Systems vs Theory)

**Response Strategy: Needs continued discussion**

We recognize concerns about the balance between systems and algorithms.

The current design reflects a belief in the importance of systems foundations, but this balance may need to be revisited or further justified.

---

## 4. Implementation Details and Resource Planning

**Response Strategy: Clarify phased implementation details**

We agree that implementation details need to be made more explicit.

Areas that should be documented more clearly include:
- Course-level scope and sequencing
- Ownership and staffing plans
- Rollout milestones over the next three years

A phased implementation document would improve confidence and reduce ambiguity.

---

## 5. Mentoring Capacity and Quality Control in Experiential Learning

**Response Strategy: Build capacity with standards and staged scaling**

We agree this is a key operational concern.

The experiential pathways should include:
- Shared mentoring expectations
- Minimum supervision and feedback standards
- Common quality-assurance checkpoints

Scaling should be gradual and tied to available mentoring capacity.

---

## 6. Course Selection Transparency (Core, including Math)

**Response Strategy: Best judgment, but more work needed**

We acknowledge the need for greater transparency in how core courses (including math) were selected.

These decisions were based on:
- Faculty judgment
- Observed industry trends
- Some engagement with published work on computing education

More systematic documentation and justification could strengthen this aspect of the proposal.

---

## 7. Enrollment Balance and Course Viability

**Response Strategy: Real concern, requires top-down solution**

We agree that enrollment imbalance is a real concern.

Addressing this would require top-down coordination and may involve:
- Rebalancing offerings
- Moving some 400-level courses to 500-level

This issue should be monitored closely moving forward.

---

## 8. Experiential Learning Credit Limit (3-Credit Cap)

**Response Strategy: Valid concern, phased approach**

We agree this is a valid concern. More extensive experiential learning could be beneficial.

However, in keeping with an evolutionary approach, we started with what we believe is feasible within the next three years.

This proposal should be viewed as a starting point, with future iterations adjusting scope and credit where capacity allows.

---

## Closing

This proposal reflects an evolutionary approach focused on near-term feasibility while leaving room for future refinement.
